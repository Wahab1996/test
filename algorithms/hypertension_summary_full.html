Comprehensive Summary of Hypertension in Adults (UpToDate 2024)

1. Definition and Classification

Hypertension is defined as:

  • ACC/AHA: ≥130/80 mmHg
  • ESC/ESH: ≥140/90 mmHg

2. Epidemiology

  • ~1.3 billion people affected globally.
  • Most common chronic medical condition.
  • Strong links to aging, obesity, diet, and family history.

3. Etiology

Primary (Essential) Hypertension

  • Multifactorial – Genetic and environmental.

Secondary Hypertension

  • Suspect with early/late onset, resistant HTN, hypokalemia.
  • Causes: CKD, RAS, hyperaldosteronism, OSA, medications.

4. Diagnosis

  • Use average of ≥2 readings on ≥2 visits.
  • Prefer ABPM or HBPM for confirmation.
  • Ensure proper technique for accuracy.

5. Clinical Evaluation

  • History: risk factors, lifestyle, medication use.
  • Physical: Fundoscopy, bruits, BMI, pulses.
  • Labs: BMP, UA, glucose, lipids, ECG.

6. Target Organ Damage

  • LVH, HF, stroke, nephropathy, retinopathy.

7. Cardiovascular Risk Assessment

  • Use ASCVD or PCE calculators.
  • Supports decisions for therapy initiation.

8. Lifestyle Modifications

InterventionEffect (approx.)

9. Pharmacologic Treatment

  • Start in Stage 2 or Stage 1 with risk factors.
  • First-line drugs: Thiazides, ACEi, ARB, CCB.
  • Beta-blockers reserved for specific indications.
  • Consider dual therapy if BP >20/10 above target.

10. Treatment Targets

PopulationTarget BP
General<130/80 mmHg
Older adults<140/90 or <130 if tolerated
CKD, Diabetes, CVD<130/80 mmHg

11. Monitoring

  • Reassess every 1–3 months.
  • Monitor for side effects and adherence.
  • Check labs if on ACEi/ARB/thiazide.

12. Resistant Hypertension

  • BP uncontrolled on 3 meds (including diuretic).
  • Evaluate for adherence and secondary causes.
  • Add mineralocorticoid antagonists (e.g., spironolactone).
